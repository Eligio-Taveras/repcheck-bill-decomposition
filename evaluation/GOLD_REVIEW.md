## hr 415327 — 18 sections → 10 groups (parser: GpoText)

**Summary:** The Entitlement Control Act of 1994 creates a mechanism to limit the growth of mandatory (direct) spending by establishing annual spending targets and requiring the President and Congress to propose offsets when spending is projected to exceed those targets. If Congress fails to enact sufficient spending reductions, the bill triggers either targeted or comprehensive sequestration — automatic across-the-board cuts to direct spending accounts. The bill also sets baseline calculation rules, exemptions, judicial review procedures, and coordinates with existing deficit-control laws.

- **[1] Sec. 1** — Gives the Act its short title and lists all of its sections in a table of contents.
- **[2] Sec. 2** — States that the Act's purpose is to create a mechanism for controlling mandatory spending and increasing accountability for it.

### Definitions & Baseline
- **[3] Sec. 3** — Defines key terms used throughout the Act, including 'direct spending,' 'sequestration,' 'current policy baseline,' and others.
- **[13] Sec. 13** — Defines how the current policy baseline is to be calculated, including assumptions about continuation of existing direct spending laws, expiring programs, and veterans' compensation.

### Spending Targets
- **[4] Sec. 4** — Directs OMB to establish annual direct spending targets for fiscal years 1995–2000, allowing growth for inflation, beneficiary changes, and a small additional allowance, with adjustments for health care reform and subsequent legislative changes.

### Presidential Budget Process
- **[5] Sec. 5** — Requires the President to include a special message with his budget proposing ways to offset projected excess direct spending, and requires that message to be introduced as a concurrent resolution in Congress.

### Congressional Reconciliation
- **[6] Sec. 6** — Requires the House Budget Committee to report a reconciliation resolution by March 15 whenever direct spending is projected to exceed targets, and sets procedures for floor consideration if the committee fails to act.
- **[7] Sec. 7** — Requires the introduction of 'spin-off' legislation after budget resolution adoption to specify allowable direct spending by functional category, and sets expedited House and Senate procedures for its consideration.

### Sequestration Mechanisms
- **[8] Sec. 8** — Establishes a targeted sequestration process that cuts direct spending within specific functional categories by a uniform percentage when spending in those categories exceeds the levels set in a spin-off law.
- **[9] Sec. 9** — Establishes a comprehensive sequestration process that applies a uniform percentage cut across all direct spending accounts when total direct spending exceeds the cap and no spin-off law is in effect.
- **[11] Sec. 11** — Sets the general rules governing how sequestrations are calculated and implemented, including requirements for permanent reductions, uniform percentage calculations, and treatment of various types of spending authority.
- **[12] Sec. 12** — Establishes the timetable and content requirements for OMB and CBO sequestration preview and final reports, and for the President's sequestration order.

### Sequestration Exemptions
- **[10] Sec. 10** — Lists the specific budget accounts, programs, and activities that are exempt from sequestration under this Act, including net interest, deposit insurance, trust fund payments, and certain constitutionally required payments.

### Interaction with Existing Budget Law
- **[14] Sec. 14** — Specifies that outlay reductions achieved under this Act's reconciliation process generally will not be counted under the pay-as-you-go rules of the Balanced Budget and Emergency Deficit Control Act, except to the extent needed to balance the PAYGO scorecard.
- **[16] Sec. 16** — Eliminates the application of the budget procedures and points of order established under House Resolution 235 (103rd Congress) for fiscal year 1995 and all future fiscal years.

### Judicial Review
- **[15] Sec. 15** — Allows Members of Congress and affected parties to sue in D.C. federal district court to challenge orders or the Act's constitutionality, requires a three-judge panel to hear such cases, and provides for direct expedited appeal to the Supreme Court within strict deadlines.

### Effective Date
- **[17] Sec. 17** — Sets the Act's effective date as the day it is signed into law, applying its provisions to fiscal year 1995 and all subsequent fiscal years.

## hjres 189669 — 14 sections → 6 groups (parser: GpoText)

**Summary:** H.J. Res. 29, the 'War Powers Amendments of 2021,' completely rewrites the existing War Powers Resolution to reassert Congress's constitutional authority over the use of military force. The bill tightens the conditions under which the President may deploy U.S. forces, strengthens consultation and reporting requirements, and adds judicial enforcement mechanisms so Congress can sue to compel compliance. It also closes loopholes by clarifying that authorization cannot be implied from appropriations, inaction, or other statutes.

### Bill Identity & Purpose
- **[0] Fallback** — This is the introductory header identifying the bill as H.J. Res. 29, introduced in the 117th Congress to amend the War Powers Resolution.
- **[1] Sec. 1** — This section gives the legislation its official short title: the 'War Powers Amendments of 2021'.
- **[2] Sec. 2** — This section states Congress's findings that the executive branch has repeatedly ignored the War Powers Resolution and declares the bill's purpose to reassert congressional war powers and prevent unauthorized military engagements.
- **[3] Sec. 3** — This section directs that the existing text of the War Powers Resolution be completely replaced with the new language that follows.
- **[4] Sec. 1** — This section restates the purpose of the War Powers Resolution and lists the key constitutional powers of Congress—declaring war, controlling spending, and making necessary laws—that underpin its war powers authority.

### Authorized Use of Force
- **[5] Sec. 2** — This section limits the introduction of U.S. Forces into hostilities to three circumstances: a declaration of war, a specific statutory authorization meeting detailed requirements (including defined threats, missions, countries, and a two-year sunset), or an emergency response to an armed attack or imminent threat.
- **[11] Sec. 8** — This section clarifies that authorization to introduce U.S. Forces into hostilities cannot be implied from appropriations laws, other statutes, or treaties unless those instruments explicitly and specifically grant such authorization, and that congressional inaction does not constitute approval of military action.

### Consultation & Reporting
- **[6] Sec. 3** — This section requires the President to consult with a defined bipartisan group of congressional leaders and committee chairs before and regularly after introducing U.S. Forces into hostilities, and specifies that genuine consultation means seeking Congress's advice before the decision is made.
- **[7] Sec. 4** — This section requires the President to submit a written report to congressional leaders within 48 hours of introducing U.S. Forces into hostilities, detailing the circumstances, legal authority, scope, duration, countries involved, and estimated costs, with follow-up reports at least every 30 days thereafter.

### Congressional Approval Process
- **[8] Sec. 5** — This section requires Congress to act within 30 days of receiving the President's report by authorizing continued use of U.S. Forces through a declaration of war or a specific joint resolution, or the President must withdraw those forces, with a possible 15-day safety extension.
- **[9] Sec. 6** — This section establishes fast-track 'privileged resolution' procedures in both the House and Senate to ensure that congressional authorizations for the use of military force receive expedited consideration and cannot be blocked by committee inaction or procedural delays.

### Judicial Enforcement
- **[10] Sec. 7** — This section grants any Member of Congress standing to sue in federal court to enforce the War Powers Resolution, bars courts from dismissing such cases as political questions, and requires courts to order the President to remove U.S. Forces if a violation is found, with direct appeal to the Supreme Court.

### Definitions & Severability
- **[12] Sec. 9** — This section defines key terms used in the resolution, including broad definitions of 'hostilities' (covering cyber attacks, intelligence sharing, and refueling), 'imminent' threat, 'introduce' (covering advisory and support roles), and 'training.'
- **[13] Sec. 10** — This section provides a standard severability clause ensuring that if any part of the resolution is found invalid, the remainder of the resolution continues in full force and effect.

### Right to Vote Act

### Voters' Access to Water

- **[143] Sec. 3806** — Clarifies that nothing in the subtitle restricts legitimate journalism or imposes additional limits on political expression by non-citizen, non-permanent-resident individuals residing in the United States.

### Paper Ballot & Voting System Security
- **[144] Sec. 3901** — Names the subtitle the 'Voter Confidence and Increased Accessibility Act of 2021.'

- **[146] Sec. 3903** — Requires voting systems to be fully accessible to voters with disabilities, including providing an equivalent, private, and independent opportunity to verify and cast a voter-verifiable paper ballot at every in-person polling location.
- **[147] Sec. 248** — Authorizes $10 million in grants to at least two eligible entities to study and develop accessible and secure remote voting systems and devices to improve ballot accessibility for individuals with disabilities, with any resulting technology to be made publicly available.
- **[148] Sec. 3904** — Requires all voter-verifiable paper ballots to be printed on durable paper capable of surviving multiple hand recounts and retaining ballot information for 22 months, and requires ballots produced by marking devices to be clearly readable by both voters and tabulation devices.

- **[150] Sec. 3906** — Bans wireless, power-line, or internet connectivity on any device used to configure, mark, cast, tabulate, or aggregate ballots, with a narrow exception for assistive technology used by voters with disabilities.
- **[151] Sec. 3907** — Sets compliance deadlines for new voting system requirements, generally requiring compliance by 2022 while allowing jurisdictions that used certain legacy paper-record systems in the 2020 election to delay until they replace those systems, and requiring those grandfathered jurisdictions to offer voters hand-marked paper ballots in the meantime.

- **[154] Sec. 298A** — Defines the permissible voting system security improvements that grant funds may be spent on, including acquiring goods from qualified vendors, cybersecurity training, vulnerability assessments, infrastructure maintenance, technical support, and enhancing voter registration system security.
- **[155] Sec. 298B** — Requires states to submit an application to the Election Assistance Commission describing their planned use of grant funds and certifying they will complete voting system security improvements within five years in order to be eligible for a grant.
- **[156] Sec. 298C** — Requires the Election Assistance Commission to submit annual reports to specified congressional committees on how grant funds under this program were used.
- **[157] Sec. 298D** — Authorizes $2.4 billion in appropriations for fiscal year 2022 and $175 million every other fiscal year through 2030 for the grant program, and establishes criteria for qualified election infrastructure vendors, including U.S. ownership or Five Eyes alliance membership, domestic assembly of voting machines, and disclosure of foreign parts sourcing and significant ownership interests.

### Small Dollar Public Financing
- **[238] Sec. 8111** — Amends the Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971 by adding a new Title V establishing a Small Dollar Financing program for House of Representatives candidates.

- **[240] Sec. 502** — Sets out the process by which a certified participating candidate requests matching fund payments, including what information must be submitted, minimum thresholds and waiting periods between requests, and a two-business-day payment deadline.
- **[241] Sec. 503** — Restricts how candidates may spend public matching funds, limiting use to authorized campaign expenditures and explicitly prohibiting use for legal fees, fines, or civil penalties.

- **[244] Sec. 512** — Specifies that a House candidate qualifies for the program by raising at least $50,000 from a minimum of 1,000 individual small-dollar contributors during the qualifying period, and directs the Division Director to create verification and audit procedures.
